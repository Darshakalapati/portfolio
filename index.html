about

Engineering clarity out of messy data

I specialize in the unglamorous, mission-critical work that keeps enterprises running: moving data safely between systems and making it dependable.

My career has been built around one deceptively hard problem: getting data from where it is to where it needs to be, intact. Over three years I've designed database structures from the ground up, lifted enormous datasets off aging legacy platforms, and shepherded them into modern systems while guarding every record's integrity, accuracy, and security along the way.

A lot of that reliability comes from automation. I lean heavily on Shell scripting to take slow, repetitive, error-prone tasks and turn them into repeatable processes that run themselves — and on tools like SQL, Control-M, and SAP to watch pipelines closely, catch failures early, and keep throughput high. When something breaks, I trace it back to the real cause rather than patching the symptom.

What ties it together is a genuinely analytical way of thinking. I notice where processes leak time, where data quietly drifts out of sync, and where a small change compounds into a big efficiency win. Finishing a Master's in Data Science at Wichita State University has extended that instinct into machine learning, statistical analysis, and data storytelling — so I can speak fluently to both the engineers who build the pipelines and the stakeholders who act on the results.

projects

Selected data work

A couple of projects that show how I approach a dataset — from cleaning and questioning it to turning it into something a decision-maker can use.

Project 01 · Research

Education Pathways & Earnings Study

A question worth answering: do students who start at a community college end up earning less than those who begin at a four-year university? I set out to measure it using years of national survey data, for a Kansas Board of Regents research initiative.

  • Framed the problem around wages, job type, and employment status, so the comparison reflected real career outcomes rather than headline averages.
  • Stitched together six survey years into one coherent dataset — reconciling schemas that changed over time, handling missing values, and dropping variables too sparse to trust.
  • Tamed the salary data by trimming implausible entries and winsorizing extremes, so a handful of outliers couldn't skew the whole story.
  • Adjusted earnings for inflation and charted distributions over time to see how the gap actually moved, not just where it landed.
  • Backed the findings with formal hypothesis testing and regression summaries, so every claim rested on statistical evidence.
  • Packaged the results into Power BI visuals that made a dense academic question legible at a glance.

Project 02 · Analytics

Reading the Airbnb Market — NYC

New York's short-term rental market is huge, noisy, and full of hidden structure. I treated a large Airbnb listings dataset less like a spreadsheet and more like a story waiting to be told — and told it two different ways for two different audiences.

  • Explored pricing, location, availability, and review patterns to find where the real opportunities and risks were hiding in the data.
  • Focused on the "so what" — translating correlations and clusters into plain-language takeaways a non-technical reader could act on.
  • Built two tailored narratives from the same analysis: a technical walkthrough for analytics leadership, and an outcome-focused version for operations and user-experience teams.
  • Framed each insight around a decision, so the work pointed toward action instead of just describing the past.
  • Used a mix of Excel, Python, and Power BI to keep the exploration fast and the final visuals clean.
